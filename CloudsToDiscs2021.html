High-mass OB stars, those larger than about 10 solar masses, are the dominant sources of feedback wherever they exist. One of the driving questions in star formation and studies of the initial mass function is what conditions allow high-mass stars to form? I will discuss the role of disks in the formation of high-mass stars and argue that, while disks are clearly present from a fairly early stage, they do not regulate accretion onto the stars. Instead, asymmetric accretion flows, which do not result in rotationally-supported structures, drive accretion, and either radiative feedback or dynamical ejection ends a massive star's growth. I will support these arguments with examples from ALMA long-baseline observations of a range of high-mass star-forming regions, and I will highlight new tools ALMA provides to study these highly obscured objects.

How does accretion end?

A star accretes gas from its surroundings.
Its final mass is set when this process stops.
What stops accretion?
  • Gas runs out (exhaustion)
  • Star is ejected
  • Gas is blown out (evacuation, feedback)
  • Another steals it (theft, fragmentation-induced starvation)
